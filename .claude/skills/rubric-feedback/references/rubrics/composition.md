# #composition

## Rubric definition

Communicate with a clear and precise style.

## Strong example — body application

You are visiting your aunt at her new house. She leaves a 5-minute long, convoluted voicemail about how to find her property, in which she tells you to drive on one road for "a while," then turn "toward the property that just got sold by Kristin, who just had a child and is moving to New York" and take a right at the Millers' farm. You have no way to know which property was recently sold or where the Millers live, and have no interest in knowing why Kristin sold her property. She then retracts some directions and suggests a detour around "the road with the Starbucks" to avoid construction. You realize en route that your cell phone's battery is dead and you can no longer communicate with her, not knowing which of the five Starbucks in your proximity she meant. You try to follow her instructions, but quickly get lost. You wish her communications had been clear, precise, parsimonious and relevant.

## Strong example — contextualization footnote

The aunt's messages exemplify a poor use of composition, as the messages are not clear and concise, using elaborate and indirect ways that could not be followed even by an attentive listener. It added irrelevant information and used diction that was not best suited for its purpose ("property" instead of "house" or "building").

## Why this pair works

- **Failure mode made concrete.** The example doesn't just assert "unclear" — it shows exactly what unclear looks like: landmarks the listener can't identify, retracted directions, ambiguous references ("the Starbucks").
- **Irrelevance is named specifically.** "Why Kristin sold her property" is flagged as irrelevant — not vaguely "extra info" but a specific piece of content that doesn't serve the communication goal.
- **Diction critique is precise.** "Property" vs. "house/building" illustrates word-choice failure with a direct comparison, not a general complaint about vocabulary.
- **The footnote diagnoses, not just describes.** It explains *why* the composition fails (elaborate and indirect, irrelevant additions, poor diction) rather than restating the definition.

## Evaluation criteria

### Strong body application
- Writing is parsimonious: no sentence contains information the reader doesn't need
- References and landmarks are ones the audience can actually use
- Word choice is suited to the purpose and audience (denotation fits; no unnecessary abstraction or jargon)
- Structure is followable without prior knowledge or back-channel clarification

### Strong footnote contextualization
- Names the specific stylistic choice made (or failure avoided)
- Explains why that choice serves clarity and precision for this audience/purpose
- Goes beyond "I wrote clearly" — points to a concrete element (a word, a sentence, a structural decision) and explains its effect

## Common weaknesses — flag these when you see them

| Weakness | What it looks like | Stronger version |
|---|---|---|
| Claim without evidence | "I wrote clearly and concisely." | "I cut the background on X (3 sentences) because the audience already knows it — keeping it would have buried the key finding." |
| Vague diction critique | "I chose precise words." | "I used 'declined 12%' instead of 'fell significantly' to give the reader an actionable number." |
| Structure not explained | No mention of why ordering or length choices were made. | "I front-loaded the recommendation because the audience reads under time pressure and may not reach the analysis section." |
| Irrelevance not addressed | Student includes background the reader doesn't need but doesn't flag it as a deliberate cut. | Name what was omitted and why omitting it serves clarity. |
