# #levelsofanalysis

## Rubric definition

Analyze characteristics or behavior of a complex system, integrating explanations found at different levels of analysis and their interactions.

## Strong example — body application

After graduation, you and five friends plan to start a nonprofit to advocate for environmental issues. You have an ambitious 5-year plan to raise money and quickly scale to hundreds of employees. Part of your planning is to ensure that your nonprofit is a great place to work, so you investigate companies that seem to be doing a good job with that.

In a 2021 survey of 70,000 companies in the USA, the happiest employees were at Adobe. In an interview about ranking #1, you learn that Adobe identified employee preferences and ideas through surveys, meetings, and focus groups, and then (1) added days each month when the entire company had days off, (2) added community-building events, including an annual diversity and inclusion event, (3) allowed each employee to take an additional 20 days off of their choice each year, (4) allowed employees to have more flexibility in their work schedules, especially in recognition of family responsibilities, and (5) reimbursed an additional $600 per year for expenses related to health and wellness.

You think about how these items worked on many levels and interacted with each other. The most important to you seem to be the community level of the company as a whole, each worker's friend or family circle, and finally, each worker. You identify how crucial a healthy working environment is for a person's overall mood based on a psychological study that you read and how feeling good after a day at work may make a person more receptive to the moods and feelings of their closest people. You clearly outlined the mechanism through which less stressful working environments are likely to influence healthy family dynamics significantly. On the other hand, you analyzed how spending more time with family and friends makes a person more well-rested (individual level) and boosts their working creativity, which improves the company's productivity overall (working community level) by providing additional examples of effective companies based on employees self-reported satisfaction with their family situation.

As your startup grew, you learned how Adobe got to #1 on employee satisfaction. In an interview, you discovered Adobe's enthusiasm for community-building events and wondered how creating a shared sense of togetherness at a group level depended on each person (individual-level) showing up happy and well-rested (biological level).

## Strong example — contextualization footnote

In your attempt to understand the phenomenon for Adobe's success you consider the individual employees' lives at multiple levels and how those might interact to support the company's productivity. First you analyze how the community building events have a beneficial effect on the employees' attitude towards the work process and how that attitude is transferred to their personal lives. Then you look at how the extra time they dedicate to their personal lives contributes to their work performance. Finally, you identify how both of those levels impact each other through the level of the individual and how the individual's perception of their situation might influence their work and family life.

## Why this pair works

- **Multiple named levels.** Community (company), social circle (family/friends), individual, and biological levels are all explicitly named — not left as vague "macro" and "micro."
- **Bidirectional interaction is shown.** Work environment improves home life AND home life improves work performance — the analysis runs in both directions, not just top-down.
- **Mechanisms are specified.** "Less stressful working environments → healthier family dynamics" and "more time with family → more well-rested → greater creativity → higher company productivity" — each link is spelled out.
- **The footnote traces the analytic path.** It doesn't just list levels; it walks through the sequence of interactions the student followed, ending at the individual as the mediating node between community and family levels.
- **Evidence is used at multiple levels.** A psychological study grounds the individual-level claim; examples of other companies ground the community-level claim.

## Evaluation criteria

### Strong body application
- Names at least two distinct, well-defined levels (e.g., individual, group, institutional, biological, societal) — not just "small scale" and "large scale"
- Shows how a phenomenon at one level influences or is explained by another level — cross-level causation, not parallel description
- Demonstrates interaction in multiple directions where relevant, not just one-way top-down or bottom-up
- Specifies the mechanism linking levels (the "how"), not just the direction

### Strong footnote contextualization
- Names the specific levels used
- Explains the interaction — not just that levels were analyzed but how they affected each other and why that cross-level move matters for understanding the system
- Identifies the individual application point: which specific levels the student chose to focus on AND why that choice illuminates the phenomenon
- Goes beyond "I analyzed multiple levels of analysis"

## Common weaknesses — flag these when you see them

| Weakness | What it looks like | Stronger version |
|---|---|---|
| Levels named but not interacting | "I analyzed the individual, group, and societal levels." | Show how group norms shape individual behavior, which then feeds back to reshape group norms. |
| One-directional only | Only shows how macro level affects micro, with no reverse | Show how micro-level changes (individual behavior) propagate back up to the system level |
| Levels confused or overlapping | "Individual" and "psychological" treated as two separate levels | Define each level clearly and make sure they are genuinely distinct analytical strata |
| Footnote just lists levels | "I applied levels of analysis by considering multiple levels." | Name the specific levels, the specific interactions, and the mechanism through which they connect |
| Analysis stays within one level | Describes individual psychology in detail without connecting it to group or institutional dynamics | Explicitly bridge upward or downward to another level with a causal claim |
| Interactions claimed but not shown | "These levels interact with each other." | Spell out the direction and mechanism: "X at the individual level propagates to Y at the group level because Z." |
