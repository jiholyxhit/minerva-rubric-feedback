# #constraints

## Rubric definition

Identify and apply constraint satisfaction as a way to solve problems.

## Strong example — body application

You are moving into a new house and need to decide where to put the furniture in your bedroom. You have an old sofa that is missing a rear leg and sits on a telephone book, so you don't want to put it in a location where it can be seen from behind. You realize, however, there is no place to put the sofa so that it is not seen from behind, and so you decide to treat this as an obstacle rather than a constraint and get the sofa fixed. You budget so that you spend less money on groceries this week so you have enough money to overcome the obstacle: in other words, fix the sofa. You also have an old bed, with a headboard that must lean against the wall. Fixing the headboard would cost way more than fixing the sofa, so it is not feasible, and you decide to keep it as a constraint to be satisfied. Additionally, you have two end tables, which must be set to either side of the headboard. And a large chair and reading lamp, which must be near each other. Once you pick a wall for the headboard (one of the constraints), the positions of the other pieces of furniture may be determined. There is only one other place where the wall can accommodate the headboard, and once it and the end tables are set up, there is only one other place large enough for the chair and lamp. Because you fixed the sofa, you can put it anywhere where space is left. The mere act of satisfying all of the constraints and overcoming obstacles simultaneously almost dictates the solution to the problem.

## Strong example — contextualization footnote

Upon facing numerous alternative choices to place furniture, the constraints that must be satisfied by a solution are identified and fulfilled sequentially to guide the furniture arrangement; in this case, satisfying all the constraints directly solved the problem.

## Why this pair works

- **Constraints vs. obstacles are distinguished.** The sofa's condition is initially a constraint but gets reclassified as an obstacle (fixable at a cost) — the student shows they understand the difference and the tradeoff.
- **Sequential satisfaction is shown.** The headboard wall choice propagates to end table positions, which propagates to the remaining space for the chair and lamp — each constraint narrows the solution space concretely.
- **The solution emerges from constraint satisfaction, not trial and error.** The body demonstrates that systematically satisfying constraints *dictates* the solution rather than the student having to exhaustively test every arrangement.
- **Feasibility is considered.** The headboard fix is ruled out as too costly — the student shows awareness that obstacles can be converted to constraints when overcoming them is infeasible.

## Evaluation criteria

### Strong body application
- Identifies specific constraints (conditions that must be satisfied) and, where relevant, distinguishes them from obstacles (conditions that can be overcome with resources)
- Shows how constraints are applied sequentially or simultaneously to narrow the solution space
- Demonstrates that satisfying constraints guides or determines the solution — not just that constraints exist
- Where relevant, considers feasibility of converting constraints to obstacles (or vice versa)

### Strong footnote contextualization
- Names the specific constraints that were identified in the problem
- Explains how satisfying those constraints shaped or determined the solution (not just "I used constraint satisfaction")
- If constraints were sequenced, says so — shows awareness that ordering can matter
- Goes beyond restating the rubric ("I identified and applied constraints")

## Common weaknesses — flag these when you see them

| Weakness | What it looks like | Stronger version |
|---|---|---|
| Constraints listed but not applied | "The constraints were X, Y, Z." with no demonstration of how they narrow the solution | Show how each constraint eliminates options or determines a choice |
| Obstacles mislabeled as constraints | A fixable condition is treated as an inviolable constraint without considering whether it could be overcome | Explicitly consider whether the condition is truly fixed or could be changed at a cost |
| No propagation shown | Each constraint is treated in isolation | Show how satisfying one constraint affects what's possible for others |
| Footnote just restates the rubric | "I identified constraints and used them to solve the problem" | Name the specific constraints, state how they interacted, and say what the solution space looked like after satisfying them |
| Solution found before constraints are applied | The student picks a solution first and then notes which constraints it satisfies | The constraint satisfaction process should *lead to* the solution, not rationalize it afterward |
