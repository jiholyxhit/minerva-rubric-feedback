# #thesis

## Rubric definition

Formulate a well-defined thesis.

## Strong example — body application

You are struck by the number of homeless people you see on the streets of San Francisco and decide to investigate further and write an article in an undergraduate journal. While you may feel outraged by the poor social and economic support you find in the city for this population, you realize that you need to do more than express your outrage in your article. You must present a clear and specific claim about conditions contributing to this problem. As such, you end the introduction of your essay with a clear, arguable, and precise thesis based on your findings: Investing in homeless shelters is merely a temporary relief to homelessness in San Francisco because doing so fails to address underlying causes in terms of housing unaffordability, low wages, and rising inflation.

## Strong example — contextualization footnote

The thesis lays out precisely what the paper will argue, provides a clear indication of the significant reasons you'll offer in support of that argument, and explains the relevance of your argument. The statement is arguable, precise, and clear.

## Why this pair works

- **The thesis is arguable.** "Investing in homeless shelters is merely temporary relief" is a contestable claim — someone could disagree. It's not a factual statement or a statement of intent.
- **The thesis is specific.** It names the city (San Francisco), the policy (shelter investment), and the three underlying causes. A reader knows exactly what the paper will and won't cover.
- **The thesis signals the paper's structure.** The three reasons (housing unaffordability, low wages, rising inflation) preview the argument's shape without over-explaining it.
- **The footnote diagnoses against criteria.** It doesn't just say "this is a thesis" — it checks arguable, precise, and clear as distinct properties, which is the actual rubric.

## Evaluation criteria

### Strong body application
- States a single, unified claim (not a question, not a statement of intent like "this paper will explore")
- The claim is **arguable**: a reasonable person could disagree with it
- The claim is **specific**: names the subject, the stance, and (if possible) the key reasons or scope
- The claim is **clear**: a reader unfamiliar with the topic understands what is being asserted
- Positioned where a reader expects a thesis (typically end of intro, or clearly signaled)

### Strong footnote contextualization
- Identifies which properties the thesis satisfies: arguable, precise, clear
- Explains *why* the specific wording achieves each property — not just "it is arguable" but what makes it arguable
- Notes any deliberate scoping decisions (why San Francisco? why these three causes?)
- Goes beyond "I wrote a thesis statement" — must engage with the quality of the claim

## Common weaknesses — flag these when you see them

| Weakness | What it looks like | Stronger version |
|---|---|---|
| Thesis is a fact, not a claim | "San Francisco has a homelessness problem." | Must be arguable: "Shelter investment fails because it ignores root causes." |
| Thesis is a question | "What causes homelessness in San Francisco?" | Rephrase as a direct claim with a stance. |
| Thesis is a statement of intent | "This paper will examine homelessness in San Francisco." | State the actual argument, not the plan. |
| Too broad | "Homelessness is caused by many factors." | Name the specific factors and the specific context. |
| Footnote restates, not justifies | "The thesis is clear and arguable." | Explain *why*: "It is arguable because a reader could reasonably defend shelter investment as effective; the paper will counter that." |
| Reasons listed but not linked | Thesis lists three causes with no "because" or logical connector. | The causal or logical link between the main claim and the reasons should be explicit. |
