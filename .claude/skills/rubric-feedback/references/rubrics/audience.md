# #audience

## Rubric definition

Tailor oral and written work by considering the situation and perspective of the people receiving it.

## Strong example — body application

You present a new product to a company that you hope will buy it in three independent meetings with the directors, engineers, and sales team. Your first meeting is with the directors. In your experience, directors are not interested in the technical details of the product, because they are more focused on making sure the product is a worthy investment. Thus, you presented a 5-minute summary of its functionality, potential revenue and time to profitability. Next, you present it to the engineers, who want an in-depth description of its "specs" and the potential problems during manufacturing, since their job relies on the product's mechanical aspects. You focus on the technical aspects of the product, taking no time to explain technical jargon knowing your audience is knowledgeable in the field. Finally, you present it to the sales team, and they ask about the most novel and noteworthy features of the product that they can use to advertise it to customers. You give them a description of the product's most useful functions and explain its most sensational aspects. You adjusted both the content and the manner in which you present it to each audience — including the length of your presentation — to meet the audience's expectations and interest.

## Strong example — contextualization footnote

The content and method of the presentation are adapted to suit each specialized target audience based on an understanding of their main interests and perspectives. For example, for the directors, who are less interested in the product details and who have time constraints, the presentation was made to be short and to focus on their area of interest: profitability.

## Why this pair works

- **Audience is named, not generic.** Directors, engineers, sales team — each with a distinct role and information need.
- **The choice is specific per audience.** 5-minute summary vs. deep technical spec walk-through vs. highlight-reel of sensational features. These are three visibly different moves.
- **Reasoning is visible in the footnote.** "Directors have time constraints and care about profitability, so the presentation was short and profit-focused." That "so" is the whole point — it's the bridge from audience analysis to content choice.
- **Body and footnote align.** The footnote points to a move that actually exists in the body (the short, profit-focused director version). If the footnote claimed something that wasn't in the body, the application would be weak even if the footnote itself sounded good.

## Evaluation criteria

### Strong body application
- Identifies at least one specific audience by role, expertise level, context, or relationship to the topic
- Shows a deliberate choice about content, tone, length, framing, level of technical detail, or structure that fits that audience
- The choice is visible in the writing itself, not just claimed

### Strong footnote contextualization
- Names the audience
- Names the specific move the student made (not "I adjusted it" but *what* was adjusted)
- Ties the move to the audience's situation, interests, or constraints with a "because" link (explicit or clearly implicit)
- Goes beyond paraphrasing the rubric definition

## Common weaknesses — flag these when you see them

| Weakness | What it looks like | Stronger version |
|---|---|---|
| Audience generic | "I wrote this for the audience." | "Written for undergraduate CS students with no ML background." |
| Choice unnamed | "I tailored the content to them." | "I removed technical jargon and used a cooking analogy for backprop." |
| Reasoning missing | "I shortened it for directors." | "I shortened it for directors because they have time constraints and care about ROI, not implementation details." |
| Body doesn't match footnote | Footnote claims tailoring, but the paragraph reads like a generic piece with no audience-specific moves. | The body should contain moves that someone reading it would recognize as aimed at a specific audience. |
| Rubric restated, not applied | "I considered my audience's perspective throughout." | Must name the perspective (who) AND the move (what) AND the link (why). |
| One audience when there should be multiple | A single paragraph treated as if it were for everyone, when the assignment clearly has different stakeholders. | Break out the moves per audience, even briefly. |
