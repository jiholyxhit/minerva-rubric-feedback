### #levelsofanalysis

**Categorization:** Habit of Mind | Complex Systems | Interacting Effectively | Interacting with Complex Systems

**Paragraph Description:**
Research conducted on different scales of analysis often reveals patterns that are not visible, or are insufficient to explain, at one level. The micro level focuses on small-scale components of a system, while the macro level examines large-scale patterns. Meso levels are between micro and macro. Findings from one level of analysis do not necessarily explain findings at another level, and this disparity must be accounted for when analyzing complex systems.

**General Example:**
An Adobe employee satisfaction nonprofit is planning outreach campaigns. The team analyzes satisfaction at the individual (micro) level by looking at survey results for each employee. They also analyze at the department (meso) level by grouping data by team. Finally, they look at company-wide (macro) trends. They notice that the macro data suggests overall high satisfaction, but the micro data reveals certain employee groups are highly dissatisfied—a finding that would be missed by only looking at the macro level.

### #shapingbehavior

**Categorization:** Habit of Mind | Complex Systems | Interacting Effectively | Negotiating and Persuading

**Paragraph Description:**
Incentives, disincentives, rewards, punishments, and choice architecture can all be used to shape behavior. Incentives encourage behavior by providing positive reinforcement, while disincentives discourage behavior through negative reinforcement or punishment. Choice architecture refers to the way choices are presented to people, which can influence their decisions without restricting options.

**General Example:**
A city government wants to reduce cigarette smoking among residents. Rather than banning smoking outright, they implement a combination of strategies: they raise taxes on cigarettes (disincentive), offer free nicotine patches at pharmacies (incentive), and redesign public spaces so that smoking areas are inconveniently located far from main pedestrian areas (choice architecture). These layered approaches shape behavior without outright prohibition.

### #systemmapping

**Categorization:** Foundational Concept | Complex Systems | Interacting Effectively | Interacting with complex systems

**Paragraph Description:**
According to Plato, there is no obvious way to "carve nature at its joints"—any complex system can be decomposed in multiple ways. System mapping involves identifying the components of a system, the relationships between those components, and the boundaries of the system. A good system map makes explicit the functional units and their interactions, revealing leverage points and potential inefficiencies.

**General Example:**
A hospital administrator creates a system map of the hospital's spending. By diagramming all departments, their costs, and their interdependencies, the administrator identifies that two departments are running nearly identical educational campaigns for patients—a redundancy that could be eliminated to save costs. The system map made this overlap visible in a way that reviewing individual budgets could not.

### #emergentproperties

**Categorization:** Foundational Concept | Complex Systems | Interacting Effectively | Understanding and predicting complex patterns.

**Paragraph Description:**
Some properties of complex systems can be easily understood as trivial combinations of parts, but emergent properties are those that arise from the interactions of components and cannot be predicted from or reduced to the properties of individual parts alone. Emergence is a key feature of complex systems, from ant colonies to economies to consciousness.

**General Example:**
At a Minerva hackathon (M3P), students who had learned Python and students who had learned JavaScript were placed in mixed groups. Individually, neither group could build a full-stack application. But through their interactions and complementary skills, the groups produced applications that neither could have created alone—an emergent outcome from the combination of different knowledge bases.

### #complexcausality

**Categorization:** Foundational Concept | Complex Systems | Interacting Effectively | Interacting with Complex Systems

**Paragraph Description:**
Complex causality involves multiple causes and interactions producing secondary, tertiary, and higher-order effects. In complex systems, effects can feed back to become causes (feedback loops), causes can interact non-linearly, and outcomes can be disproportionate to their initial triggers. Understanding complex causality is essential for diagnosing problems and designing effective interventions.

**General Example:**
Analysts studying San Francisco homelessness identify multiple interacting causes: rising rents, mental health crises, inadequate shelter capacity, drug addiction, and insufficient social services. These causes interact—mental illness can lead to job loss, which leads to inability to pay rent, which leads to homelessness, which worsens mental illness. Any single-cause explanation or single-intervention solution misses this complexity.

### #networks

**Categorization:** Foundational Concept | Complex Systems | Interacting Effectively | Understanding and predicting complex patterns

**Paragraph Description:**
Network analysis examines relationships and connections among agents (nodes) through edges (connections). Networks can reveal patterns of influence, information flow, and vulnerability. Key metrics include degree centrality (number of connections), betweenness centrality (how often a node lies on shortest paths), and clustering coefficients. Understanding network structure helps predict behavior and design interventions.

**General Example:**
An intern wants to spread a new idea through their office but doesn't know whom to approach first. By mapping informal communication networks—who talks to whom at lunch, whose advice people seek—they identify a "hub" person who connects many otherwise disconnected colleagues. By convincing this hub person first, the intern efficiently spreads the idea throughout the organization.

### #systemdynamics

**Categorization:** Foundational Concept | Complex Systems | Interacting Effectively | Interacting within Complex Systems

**Paragraph Description:**
Phase spaces describe and predict changes in complex systems over time. System dynamics involves stocks (accumulations), flows (rates of change), and feedback loops (reinforcing or balancing). By modeling these elements, one can understand how systems evolve, identify tipping points, and anticipate unintended consequences of interventions.

**General Example:**
The mayor of Lakeville, a town that had been economically floundering, used system dynamics thinking to understand why previous economic interventions had failed. By mapping reinforcing loops (e.g., unemployment leads to decreased spending, which leads to more business closures and more unemployment) and identifying leverage points, the mayor designed an intervention that broke the negative reinforcing loop, eventually leading the town to flourish economically.

### #negotiate

**Categorization:** Habit of Mind | Complex Systems | Interacting Effectively | Negotiating and Persuading

**Paragraph Description:**
Effective negotiation involves clearly identifying interests (underlying needs and desires) and areas of common ground, as distinct from positions (stated demands). Interest-based negotiation (as opposed to positional bargaining) opens up creative solutions that satisfy both parties. Key strategies include active listening, separating people from problems, and generating options for mutual gain.

**General Example:**
A factory wants to build a new facility. The city government objects on environmental grounds. Rather than treating it as a zero-sum dispute, the negotiating teams identify shared interests: the city wants clean air and economic growth; the factory wants to operate efficiently and maintain community goodwill. By focusing on these shared interests, they reach an agreement that includes the factory installing advanced emissions controls in exchange for streamlined permitting.

### #persuasion

**Categorization:** Habit of Mind | Multimodal Communications | Negotiating and Persuading

**Paragraph Description:**
Persuasion techniques appeal to humans' emotional range as well as to their reason. Aristotle's rhetorical appeals—ethos (credibility), pathos (emotion), and logos (logic)—provide a classical framework. Modern persuasion research highlights the importance of framing, social proof, reciprocity, scarcity, and authority. Effective persuasion tailors the approach to the audience and context.

**General Example:**
Someone wants to convince their family to use reusable coffee cups instead of disposable ones. They use pathos by sharing images of ocean plastic pollution and connecting it to the family's love of the beach. They use logos by calculating the annual cost savings of reusable cups. They use ethos by noting that a respected family friend has already made the switch. By combining these appeals, they successfully shift the family's behavior.

### #ethicalconsiderations

**Categorization:** Habit of Mind | Complex Systems | Interacting Effectively | Resolving Ethical Problems

**Paragraph Description:**
Many decisions involve ethical considerations—questions about what is right, fair, or good. Ethical frameworks such as consequentialism (focus on outcomes), deontology (focus on duties and rules), and virtue ethics (focus on character) provide different lenses for evaluating decisions. Identifying affected stakeholders, weighing harms and benefits, and considering rights and duties are all part of ethical analysis.

**General Example:**
A game design studio is developing a mobile game called WhoSolvedItFirst and considering implementing microtransactions that would give paying players significant advantages. The team identifies ethical considerations: the mechanic may exploit children who cannot understand the financial implications, create unfair advantages based on wealth rather than skill, and undermine the game's educational value. Applying consequentialist reasoning, they redesign the monetization to avoid pay-to-win mechanics.

### #ethicalcourage

**Categorization:** Habit of Mind | Complex Systems | Interacting Effectively | Resolving Ethical Problems

**Paragraph Description:**
It is often challenging to figure out the right thing to do in complex situations, and even harder to act on that judgment when facing social pressure, authority, or personal cost. Ethical courage involves recognizing when ethical action is required, determining the right course of action, and having the fortitude to act on that judgment despite potential costs or resistance.

**General Example:**
A quiet student in a residence hall is being targeted by anonymous bullying through an app. Most students are aware of the situation but say nothing because they fear social backlash. One student decides it is ethically necessary to speak up, reports the behavior to residential advisors, and publicly advocates for a community norm against anonymous harassment—despite the social risk. This action shifts the community's norms and stops the bullying.

### #conformity

**Categorization:** Habit | Complex Systems | Interacting Effectively | Working With Others

**Paragraph Description:**
Humans have a "herd instinct"—a tendency to conform to group norms and behaviors. Conformity can be informational (following others because they seem to have better information) or normative (following others to gain social acceptance). Understanding conformity is crucial for predicting social behavior and designing effective interventions that leverage or counteract it.

**General Example:**
Students in a residence hall want to improve kitchen cleanliness. Rather than posting rules or assigning blame, they launch a norm-change campaign. They post signs showing that "most students in this hall clean up after themselves within 15 minutes" (using descriptive norms) and leave thank-you notes when the kitchen is clean. By making the prosocial norm visible and positive, they leverage conformity to improve behavior.

### #emotionaliq

**Categorization:** Habit | Complex Systems | Interacting Effectively | Working with others

**Paragraph Description:**
Emotional intelligence (EQ) is the ability to be aware of, evaluate, and manage one's own emotions and the emotions of others. High EQ involves perceiving emotions accurately, using emotions to facilitate thought, understanding emotional meaning and causes, and managing emotions effectively. EQ is a stronger predictor of professional success in collaborative environments than IQ alone.

**General Example:**
A project manager notices that afternoon team meetings are consistently unproductive and contentious. Recognizing that mid-afternoon hunger ("hanger") may be affecting team members' emotional states and decision-making quality, they start providing popcorn at afternoon meetings. This small intervention improves emotional regulation, reduces conflict, and significantly improves meeting outcomes.

### #powerdynamics

**Categorization:** Habit | Complex Systems | Interacting Effectively | Working With Others

**Paragraph Description:**
Power is one's ability to get others to do what they otherwise wouldn't. Power can be formal (authority from position) or informal (influence from expertise, relationships, or information). Power dynamics shape interactions within organizations and social systems. Understanding power involves recognizing its sources, how it flows, how it can be used, and how it can be resisted or redistributed.

**General Example:**
At a participatory budgeting meeting, community members are nominally empowered to allocate city funds. However, a facilitator observes that certain well-organized groups with prior experience dominate the discussion, while newer community members and non-English speakers are effectively sidelined. By recognizing these informal power dynamics, the facilitator restructures the process—using small group discussions, translation services, and anonymous voting—to more equitably distribute decision-making power.

**Paragraph Description:**
Humans generally form overly optimistic assessments of their own characteristics, knowledge, and abilities—a phenomenon known as illusory superiority. Self-awareness involves accurately assessing one's own skills, biases, emotional states, and impact on others. ### #psychologicalexplanation

**Categorization:** Foundational Concept | Complex Systems | Thinking Critically | Analyzing Decision

**Paragraph Description:**
Within social systems, each human is a complex agent whose behavior can be explained by psychological theories. Psychological explanations draw on theories of cognition, emotion, motivation, and social influence to account for why people behave as they do. Major frameworks include Beck's Cognitive Model (which links situations, thoughts, emotions, and behaviors), self-determination theory, and social learning theory.

**General Example:**
A nonprofit employee is confused why a new colleague seems hostile. The employee later learns the colleague saw them eating the colleague's labeled yogurt on the first day—an accident the employee didn't notice. Using Beck's Cognitive Model, the employee understands: the situation (missing yogurt) triggered the thought "this person disrespects me," which triggered the emotion of anger, which produced avoidant hostile behavior. The employee buys the colleague a 6-pack of yogurt and apologizes, resolving the misunderstanding.

### #multimedia

**Categorization:** Foundational Concept | Multimodal Communications | Using Nonverbal Communication

**Paragraph Description:**
Visual, aural, spoken, and written modalities may be combined in different ways to create multimodal communications. Each modality has distinctive affordances—things it does particularly well—and combining modalities can produce effects that no single modality could achieve alone. Effective multimodal communication requires understanding how different modalities interact and reinforce or undermine each other.

**General Example:**
An anti-drunk-driving TV commercial begins with a festive scene—upbeat music, bright colors, laughing friends at a party. Midway through, the music cuts to silence, the colors drain to grey, and the scene shifts to a tragic car accident. The contrast between the two modalities (audio and visual) working together creates an emotional impact far greater than either a text warning or images alone could achieve.

### #designthinking

**Categorization:** Habit of Mind | Multimodal Communications | Thinking Creatively | Solving Problems

**Paragraph Description:**
Design thinking is a human-centered approach to problem solving that emphasizes empathy with users, iterative prototyping, and learning from failure. The design thinking process typically involves: Empathize (understand user needs), Define (articulate the problem), Ideate (generate solutions), Prototype (build rough versions), and Test (evaluate with real users). Design thinking is especially valuable for complex problems with ill-defined parameters.

**General Example:**
A county government wants to announce a new park to residents. Rather than immediately creating a final announcement poster, a design team interviews residents about how they currently learn about local events, creates three rough poster prototypes with different visual styles and information hierarchies, tests each with a sample of residents, and iterates based on feedback. The final poster is dramatically more effective than a first-draft approach would have been.

### #context

**Categorization:** Foundational Concept | Multimodal Communications | Thinking Critically | Evaluating Claims

**Paragraph Description:**
To understand an expressive work, it is important to understand its context—including the historical, cultural, biographical, and generic contexts in which it was created and received. Context shapes intended meaning, and the same work can mean very different things in different contexts. Stripping a work from its context (decontextualization) often produces fundamental misunderstandings.

**General Example:**
Sia's song "Chandelier" is frequently played at parties because of its upbeat, anthemic sound. However, understanding its biographical context—Sia wrote it about her own struggle with alcoholism, describing the reckless behavior that accompanies addiction—reveals that it is a deeply sad song about self-destruction, not a celebration. Without this context, listeners fundamentally misread the work.

### #critique

**Categorization:** Habit of Mind | Multimodal Communications | Thinking Critically | Evaluating Claims

**Paragraph Description:**
Understanding a text or communication requires deep examination of its claims, assumptions, ambiguities, internal consistency, and relationship to evidence. Critique is not merely identifying flaws but involves careful analysis of how well a work achieves its stated or implied purposes. Effective critique considers both strengths and weaknesses and is grounded in specific textual evidence.

**General Example:**
A political science student reads a paper arguing that Indian voter behavior is primarily driven by caste identity. They conduct a careful critique: identifying the paper's central claim and evidence, examining what assumptions the argument relies on, noting alternative explanations the author doesn't address, questioning the data sources, and assessing whether the evidence actually supports the strong causal claim being made. The critique reveals that correlation evidence is being presented as if it demonstrates causation.

### #evidencebased

**Categorization:** Habit of Mind | Thinking Critically to Evaluate Justifications | Thinking Critically | Evaluating Justifications

**Paragraph Description:**
Written communication intended to make a specific point should clearly present relevant and persuasive evidence. Evidence-based reasoning involves selecting evidence that is directly relevant to the claim, accurately representing the evidence (including its limitations), and logically connecting the evidence to the conclusion. Different types of evidence (anecdotal, expert opinion, statistical, experimental) have different strengths and appropriate uses.

**General Example:**
A school administrator proposes a new math curriculum. Rather than relying on testimonials, they support the proposal with evidence: two interventional studies showing significant learning gains, a comparison of test scores in cities that adopted similar curricula, and expert endorsements from mathematics education researchers. They also acknowledge a limitation—one study was conducted in a different demographic context. The evidence-based presentation is far more persuasive than opinion alone.

### #sourcequality

**Categorization:** Habit of Mind | Multimodal Communications | Evaluating Justifications

**Paragraph Description:**
Sources of information can be scrutinized in terms of currency (how recent), relevance (how directly applicable), authority (expertise and credentials of author), accuracy (how well supported by evidence), and purpose (why it was written—to inform, persuade, sell, etc.). The CRAAP test (Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, Purpose) provides a systematic framework for evaluating source quality.

**General Example:**
A student researching sleep and health begins with a friend's claim, moves to a Wikipedia article, finds a health blog linked from Wikipedia, and eventually locates the peer-reviewed journal article the blog was based on. Applying source quality criteria: the friend's claim has no documented authority or accuracy; Wikipedia is current and relevant but lacks primary authority; the blog is written for a general audience with commercial purposes; the peer-reviewed article has high authority and documented methodology. The student uses only the primary source for their paper.

### #audience

**Categorization:** Habit of Mind | Multimodal Communications | Communicating Effectively | Using Language

**Paragraph Description:**
Different audiences have different background knowledge, interests, and goals. Effective communication requires two crucial steps: first, characterizing the audience (who they are, what they know, what they care about, what they need); and second, tailoring the communication to that specific audience (adjusting vocabulary, depth, emphasis, examples, and format accordingly).

A product manager presents a new software feature to three different audiences in one day. For the executive directors, they prepare a 5-minute summary focused on strategic value and ROI. For the engineering team, they present detailed technical specifications and implementation challenges. For the sales team, they highlight novel features and competitive advantages using customer-facing language. The same product, communicated three completely different ways.

### #composition

**Categorization:** Habit of Mind | Multimodal Communications | Communicating Effectively | Using Language

**Paragraph Description:**
Effective communication requires a clear and precise style, using the fewest words necessary (parsimony) to convey meaning accurately. Good composition avoids passive voice (which obscures agency and weakens prose), stilted diction (overly formal or unnatural language), redundancy, and unnecessary qualifications. Parsimony is not about being brief for its own sake, but about eliminating everything that does not serve the communication.

**General Example:**
A student receives a voicemail from their aunt asking for directions to a dinner party. The voicemail is 5 minutes long, covers irrelevant backstory about how the aunt learned about the party, uses vague language, circles back to information already given, and ends without actually specifying the address. Applying composition principles, a follow-up text message conveys all necessary information—address, arrival time, parking instructions—in four clear sentences.

### #connotation

**Categorization:** Habit of Mind | Multimodal Communications | Communicating Effectively | Using Language

**Paragraph Description:**
Connotation refers to the emotion or feeling(s) that the words, tone, and style of a communication imply—as distinct from their literal (denotative) meaning. Word choice powerfully shapes audience response. Synonyms often have very different connotations: "forceful" versus "aggressive," "thrifty" versus "cheap," "confident" versus "arrogant." Skilled communicators choose words whose connotations reinforce their intended message.

**General Example:**
Activists campaigning against police brutality carefully analyze their word choices. They debate whether to describe certain police actions as "forceful" (which connotes necessary strength in the face of resistance) versus "strong" (which connotes power without the negative aggressiveness connotation they want to invoke). They ultimately choose language whose connotations most accurately convey their critique of unjustified aggression.

### #organization

**Categorization:** Habit of Mind | Multimodal Communications | Communicating Effectively | Using Language

**Paragraph Description:**
Effective communication must be organized in a way that will be easy for the intended audience to understand and remember. Organizational strategies include using clear topic sentences, logical sequencing (chronological, by importance, compare/contrast, problem/solution), signposting (explicit transitions that guide the reader), and structure that matches the communication's purpose. A reverse outline—outlining a draft after writing it—can reveal organizational problems.

Reorganizing the article based on the reverse outline significantly improves reader comprehension and persuasive impact.

### #thesis

**Categorization:** Habit of Mind | Multimodal Communications | Communicating Effectively | Using Language

**Paragraph Description:**
A thesis statement should be Concise, Arguable, Substantial, Precise, set up Evidence, and Relevant—remembered as CASPER. A thesis is not a statement of fact (which cannot be argued) but a claim that requires evidence and reasoning to support. A strong thesis tells readers what to expect from the piece and establishes why the argument matters.

**General Example:**
A journalist writes an article about San Francisco homelessness. Their first thesis draft—"San Francisco has a homelessness problem"—fails the CASPER test: it is not arguable (everyone agrees) and not substantial. After revision, their thesis becomes: "Investing in temporary shelters provides only superficial relief for San Francisco's homelessness crisis; without addressing root causes—housing affordability and mental health services—shelter expansion will fail to reduce chronic homelessness." This version is arguable, substantial, precise, and sets up the evidence the article will present.

The revised delivery is dramatically more effective.

### #medium

**Categorization:** Foundational Concept | Multimodal Communications | Using Nonverbal Communication

**Paragraph Description:**
Effective communication requires paying close attention to medium—the channel or format through which a message is delivered—and the use of medium-specific tools. Each medium (film, music, text, speech, image) has its own conventions, affordances, and constraints. This sophisticated use of music as a medium-specific tool would be impossible to replicate in print.

### #algorithms

**Categorization:** Foundational Concept | Formal Analyses | Thinking Creatively | Solving Problems

**Paragraph Description:**
The steps of a proper algorithm are well-ordered, clear, unambiguous, and effective—they always produce a correct result when applied to valid inputs. Algorithms provide systematic procedures for solving problems or accomplishing tasks. Understanding algorithms involves specifying inputs and outputs, identifying stopping conditions, analyzing efficiency, and verifying correctness.

**General Example:**
A residence hall administrator needs to assign students to rooms. They design an algorithm that takes as input a list of students with their preferences and constraints (e.g., desired roommate, accessibility needs), processes students in priority order, checks each candidate room against all constraints, assigns the first valid match, and continues until all students are assigned or no valid assignment exists. The algorithm guarantees a consistent and fair process, unlike ad hoc decision-making.

### #optimization

**Paragraph Description:**
Optimization involves determining the relevant local or global extrema (maximum or minimum values) within a set of solutions, subject to constraints. Optimization problems require specifying an objective function (what to optimize), decision variables (what can be changed), and constraints (boundaries on the solution space). Techniques include calculus-based methods, linear programming, and heuristic approaches like genetic algorithms.

**General Example:**
A college student wants to minimize grocery costs while meeting nutritional requirements. They specify an objective function (minimize total cost), decision variables (quantities of each food item), and constraints (minimum daily calories, protein, vitamins; maximum budget). Using a genetic algorithm, they find the optimal food combination—a solution that satisfies all nutritional constraints at minimal cost.

### #confidenceintervals

**Categorization:** Habit of Mind | Formal Analyses | Thinking Critically | Analyzing Problems

**Paragraph Description:**
A confidence interval specifies upper and lower values that are likely to bracket an unknown population value with a specified level of confidence (commonly 95%). Confidence intervals communicate both the estimated value and the uncertainty around that estimate. Overlapping confidence intervals suggest that apparent differences may not be statistically meaningful; non-overlapping intervals suggest the difference is likely real.

**General Example:**
Mark is trying to decide whether to bet on an election outcome based on approval ratings. Candidate A has 51% approval with a 95% CI of [48%, 54%]; Candidate B has 49% with a CI of [46%, 52%]. Mark recognizes that the overlapping confidence intervals mean the apparent difference is not statistically significant—he cannot conclude that Candidate A is genuinely ahead, and decides not to bet.

### #correlation

**Categorization:** Habit of Mind | Formal Analyses | Thinking Critically | Analyzing Data

**Paragraph Description:**
Correlation is a statistical relationship that describes the degree to which two variables are associated. The correlation coefficient (r) ranges from -1 (perfect negative correlation) to +1 (perfect positive correlation), with 0 indicating no linear relationship. Correlation does not imply causation—a third variable may cause both, or the relationship may be coincidental. Scatter plots are the primary visual tool for exploring correlations.

**General Example:**
A business analyst at a multilateral development bank examines whether project duration is correlated with media coverage (measured by number of articles). They create a scatter plot and calculate r = 0.65, indicating a moderate positive correlation. However, they caution against assuming causation: longer projects may involve more phases that attract coverage, but media attention may also extend projects by creating accountability and scrutiny.

### #descriptivestats

**Categorization:** Habit of Mind | Formal Analyses | Thinking Critically | Analyzing Data

**Paragraph Description:**
Descriptive statistics include measures of location—mean (arithmetic average), median (middle value), and mode (most frequent value)—and measures of spread—standard deviation (average deviation from mean) and range (maximum minus minimum). Understanding which measure is most appropriate depends on the data's distribution. In skewed distributions, the median is more representative than the mean; standard deviation quantifies variability and risk.

**General Example:**
A student choosing between careers as a physician and as a ship's first mate examines salary data. Physicians have a high mean salary but a low median—because a small number of specialists earn extremely high salaries, skewing the mean upward. First mates have a higher median salary than the physician median, and much lower variance. For someone who wants predictable earnings rather than a chance at extreme wealth, the descriptive statistics favor the maritime career.

### #distributions

**Categorization:** Foundational Concept | Formal Analyses | Thinking Critically | Analyzing Data

**Paragraph Description:**
The distribution of a variable is a function that maps all possible values to how often they occur. Key distributions include the normal (bell-curve) distribution, the binomial distribution, and the Poisson distribution. The Central Limit Theorem states that the sampling distribution of the mean approaches normality as sample size increases, regardless of the population distribution—a foundational result enabling statistical inference.

**General Example:**
An engineer must determine the probability that an elevator will exceed its 700kg weight limit when carrying 9 people. Individual passenger weights are not normally distributed, but by the Central Limit Theorem, the sum of 9 weights will be approximately normally distributed. The engineer calculates the mean and standard deviation of the sum distribution and determines the probability that it exceeds 700kg—informing whether the elevator's capacity rating is appropriate.

### #probability

**Categorization:** Foundational Concept | Formal Analyses | Thinking Critically | Analyzing Data

**Paragraph Description:**
Probability specifies how likely it is that a specific event will occur. Different interpretations include frequentist (probability as long-run frequency) and Bayesian (probability as degree of belief, updated by evidence). Bayes' theorem provides the formal method for updating probabilities in light of new evidence: P(A|B) = P(B|A) x P(A) / P(B). Understanding base rates is crucial for avoiding common probability errors.

**General Example:**
During an annual physical, a patient tests positive for meningitis. The test has 95% sensitivity and 95% specificity. The doctor applies Bayes' theorem: given the very low base rate of meningitis in the general population (1 in 100,000), and despite the positive test, the patient's actual probability of having meningitis is less than 1%. The doctor orders a confirmatory test rather than immediately treating—demonstrating the importance of base rates in probability reasoning.

### #regression

**Categorization:** Foundational Concept | Formal Analyses | Thinking Critically | Analyzing Data

**Paragraph Description:**
A regression function represents a model for the relationship between a dependent variable and one or more independent variables. Simple linear regression estimates the best-fit line through a scatter plot. Multiple regression includes multiple predictors. R-squared indicates the proportion of variance in the dependent variable explained by the model. Residual analysis checks whether model assumptions are met.

**General Example:**
A public health consultant wants to predict BMI from lifestyle variables. A simple regression using only exercise minutes explains 40% of variance (R²=0.40). Adding dietary variables—daily carbohydrate, protein, and fiber intake—to a multiple regression model improves explanatory power dramatically (R²=0.89), indicating that diet and exercise together are much more informative than exercise alone.

### #significance

**Categorization:** Foundational Concept | Formal Analyses | Thinking Critically | Analyzing Data

**Paragraph Description:**
Tests of statistical significance tell us when a pattern in a sample is likely due to chance versus a genuine effect in the population. A p-value below a threshold (commonly 0.05) indicates statistical significance. However, statistical significance must be distinguished from practical significance—a very large sample can make even trivially small effects statistically significant. Effect sizes (such as Cohen's d) quantify practical significance.

**General Example:**
A clinical trial of a new headache treatment finds a statistically significant reduction in pain duration (p=0.03). However, the actual reduction is only 15 minutes on average, and Cohen's d=0.1 indicates a very small effect size. The treatment is statistically significant (unlikely to be due to chance) but practically insignificant (the reduction is too small to matter to patients or justify the cost of the medication).

### #decisiontrees

**Categorization:** Habit of Mind | Formal Analyses | Thinking Critically | Analyzing Decisions

**Paragraph Description:**
A decision tree replaces a large, complex decision with a series of smaller decisions made in sequence. Decision nodes represent choices; chance nodes represent uncertain outcomes with associated probabilities. Expected value is calculated by multiplying outcomes by their probabilities. Decision trees make explicit the structure of sequential decisions and can be "folded back" to determine the optimal strategy.

**General Example:**
A person deciding between a beach or mountains weekend trip considers purchasing a $10 weather app. They construct two decision trees—one with the app, one without—calculating expected enjoyment values based on weather probabilities. The app predicts beach weather, they go to the beach, but the weather turns horrible. Post-hoc analysis reveals whether the app's value (given its prediction accuracy) justified the cost.

### #utility

**Categorization:** Foundation Concept | Formal Analyses | Thinking Critically | Analyzing Decisions

**Paragraph Description:**
A utility function serves as a mathematical model describing preferences by mapping outcome values to satisfaction (utility). Utility theory underpins rational decision-making under uncertainty. Common deviations from purely rational utility maximization include loss aversion (losses hurt more than equivalent gains feel good), risk aversion (preferring certain outcomes to risky ones with equal expected value), and probability distortion (overweighting small probabilities).

**General Example:**
A friend buys an extended warranty on a $200 pair of headphones, even though the warranty is statistically a bad deal. Utility theory explains the decision: loss aversion means the potential loss of $200 feels worse than the expected value calculation suggests; risk aversion makes the certain (if small) cost of the warranty preferable to the uncertain (if unlikely) loss of $200; and probability distortion leads the friend to overestimate the likelihood of headphone failure.

### #gametheory

**Categorization:** Foundational Concept | Formal Analyses | Thinking Critically | Analyzing Problems

**Paragraph Description:**
Game theory provides a framework to study decision-making behavior among multiple interacting intelligent agents. Key elements include: players (the decision-makers), strategies (possible actions), payoffs (outcomes resulting from strategy combinations), and equilibria (stable states where no player can improve their outcome by unilaterally changing strategy). The Nash equilibrium is the most important solution concept.

**General Example:**
Two pizza restaurants are deciding whether to share their secret recipes. In a single-round game, this resembles a Prisoner's Dilemma: both restaurants have a dominant strategy to share false/incorrect recipes, resulting in both receiving worse outcomes than if they had cooperated (Nash equilibrium = mutual defection). In a repeated game, however, the threat of future retaliation makes cooperation rational, and both restaurants share genuine recipes, enjoying mutual benefit.

### #variables

**Categorization:** Habit of Mind | Formal Analyses | Thinking Critically | Analyzing Problems

**Paragraph Description:**
Identifying and classifying relevant variables, examining relationships between them, and considering how they can be measured and manipulated is fundamental to empirical and formal analysis. Key variable types include: independent (manipulated/predictor), dependent (measured/outcome), confounding (related to both independent and dependent variables, potentially causing spurious correlations), and mediating/moderating variables.

**General Example:**
A researcher studying tipping behavior in a restaurant identifies server friendliness as the independent variable (the presumed cause) and tip percentage as the dependent variable (the measured outcome). They also identify potential confounders: table size (larger parties may tip differently), time of day (dinner vs. lunch patrons have different demographics), and whether alcohol was ordered. By identifying and controlling for these confounders, they can better isolate the effect of friendliness on tipping.

### #deduction

**Categorization:** Foundational Concept | Thinking Critically to Evaluate Justifications

**Paragraph Description:**
Deductive reasoning uses a set of propositions as premises to reach a conclusion that follows necessarily from those premises. A deductive argument is valid if the conclusion must be true given the premises are true, and sound if valid AND all premises are actually true. Common deductive argument forms include modus ponens, modus tollens, and disjunctive syllogism.

**General Example:**
A person needs to know if the bank is open Saturday. They know: either the bank or the café is open on Saturday (but not both); the café is open on Saturday; therefore (by disjunctive syllogism), the bank is not open on Saturday. This valid deductive argument guarantees the conclusion given the truth of the premises—it does not merely make the conclusion probable.

### #fallacies

**Categorization:** Foundational Concept | Formal Analyses | Thinking Critically | Evaluating Claims

**Paragraph Description:**
Formal fallacies result from errors in argument structure (the conclusion does not follow even if the premises are true). Informal fallacies result from issues with the content of propositions (premises that are misleading, irrelevant, or ambiguous). Common informal fallacies include: straw man (misrepresenting an opponent's argument), ad hominem (attacking the person rather than the argument), appeal to authority, and false dichotomy.

**General Example:**
During a presidential debate, a candidate proposes a universal healthcare plan. The opponent responds by calling it "socialism" and attacking socialist economic systems—rather than engaging with the actual proposal. This is a straw man fallacy: by replacing the candidate's nuanced healthcare proposal with a caricatured position, the opponent avoids engaging with the real argument. Identifying this fallacy helps evaluate the quality of the debate.

### #induction

**Categorization:** Foundational Concept | Thinking Critically to Evaluate Justifications

**Paragraph Description:**
Induction involves reasoning from specific instances, observations, or experiences to a conclusion that is likely to be true. Unlike deduction, inductive conclusions are not guaranteed by the premises—they are made probable. The strength of an inductive argument depends on the number and representativeness of the instances, and on the absence of known counterexamples. Induction underlies scientific reasoning and empirical generalization.

**General Example:**
A friend named Kate claims that all Kpop fans love bubble tea, based on having met two Kpop fans who love bubble tea. This inductive argument is very weak—the sample size is tiny and almost certainly not representative. To strengthen the argument, Kate would need data from many Kpop fans, sampled in a way that avoids selection bias, before making such a broad generalization.

### #estimation

**Categorization:** Foundational Concept | Formal Analyses | Thinking Critically | Evaluating Claims

**Paragraph Description:**
Estimation involves making reasonable approximations of quantities that are difficult or impossible to measure directly. Common techniques include: powers of 10 (for order-of-magnitude estimates), round numbers (simplifying calculations while preserving accuracy), and establishing limits (minimum and maximum values that bracket the true answer). Fermi estimation—breaking complex questions into tractable sub-questions—is a key skill.

**General Example:**
To estimate the number of hotels in Los Angeles, a student applies Fermi estimation: LA has about 4 million residents; roughly 5-10% are tourists/visitors at any time (200,000-400,000 people); hotels average 20-1,000 rooms (estimate 150 rooms); with an occupancy rate of roughly 70%, each hotel serves about 100-150 guests. Calculation: 300,000 guests / 125 guests per hotel = approximately 2,400 hotels. Adjusting for the wide range of assumptions yields an estimate of approximately 1,050 hotels. The actual number is 1,008.

### #comparisongroups

**Paragraph Description:**
Comparison groups are used to test alternative conditions or control for extraneous variables. ### #interventionalstudy

**Categorization:** Foundational Concept | Empirical Analysis | Thinking Creatively | Applying Research Methods

**Paragraph Description:**
A true experiment has independent variables that are manipulated (interventions) and dependent variables that are measured as outcomes. True experiments with random assignment allow causal inference. Elements include: treatment groups (receiving the intervention), control groups (not receiving the intervention or receiving a placebo), randomization, and blinding (to reduce bias). External validity concerns how broadly findings generalize.

**General Example:**
Researchers studying antibiotic resistance design an interventional study with three intervention groups (different antibiotic dosing regimens), a placebo group, and a natural history group (no treatment). The design allows strong causal inference about which dosing regimen best minimizes resistance development.

### #interviewsurvey

**Categorization:** Foundational Concept | Empirical Analyses | Thinking Creatively | Applying Research Methods

**Paragraph Description:**
Much primary research involves interviewing or surveying human participants. **General Example:**

The revised survey yields much more valid and actionable data.

### #observationalstudy

**Categorization:** Foundational Concept | Empirical Analyses | Thinking Creatively | Applying Research Methods

**Paragraph Description:**
In an observational study, the researcher measures variables without manipulating them. Observational studies are designed to be careful, systematic, and unbiased—avoiding selective attention to confirming evidence, using standardized protocols, and considering alternative explanations. Types include cross-sectional (one time point), longitudinal (multiple time points), and naturalistic observation.

**General Example:**

They also control for potential confounders like time of day and subject matter. The systematic observation either confirms or refutes the initial impression.

**Paragraph Description:**
Sampling involves assessing characteristics of a subset (sample) in order to estimate those characteristics of the full population. ### #studyreplication

To determine how broadly applicable conclusions are, one can replicate a study in different contexts, with different populations, using different methods, or at different times. ### #hypothesisdevelopment

**Categorization:** Foundational Concept | Empirical Analyses | Thinking Creatively | Applying Research Methods

**Paragraph Description:**
Scientific research begins with observations, which then must be organized to suggest underlying patterns of regularity. Such patterns in turn suggest hypotheses about the nature of the factors that may give rise to these patterns in the data. As science progresses, scientists formulate and use theories to develop further hypotheses and design additional studies. A well-formed hypothesis requires one to appreciate the links between data, theories, and models. Hypothesis-driven research is essential to the iterative cycles of data collection and theorizing that forms the core of the scientific method.

**General Example:**
You read a story about Blue Moon butterflies in the Samoan Islands. The butterflies were attacked by a parasite that destroyed only male embryos and resulted in males being only 1% of the population. But after 10 generations (about 1 year) males are now 40% of the population. Based on these facts, you come up with two competing explanations: (a) an extinction event could have led to the sudden disappearance of the parasite, which would explain the sudden resurgence of male butterflies, or (b) male butterflies developed a resistance against the parasites, which might also explain such resurgence. Hypothesis (b) also stems from your knowledge of genetics and the plausibility of developing resistance to particularly harmful species through genetic mutations coupled with natural selective pressures. You discuss predictions that stem from these two explanations, specifically that in case (a) most parasites would be gone, and in case (b) that the parasites might still be present in butterflies that developed resistances. Finally, you make the connection between study design, testing the mentioned predictions, and deciding which of your hypothesis is corroborated. (And in fact, researchers found that the parasite was still present and that currently, males all carried the mutation that allowed them to survive the parasite's attack.)

### #modeling

**Categorization:** Foundational Concept | Empirical Analyses | Thinking Creatively | Facilitating Discovery

**Paragraph Description:**
Models are simplified representations of reality. These representations can take many forms, including conceptual models, physical models, mathematical models, and more. Models have crucial applications in science, allowing us to understand systems, explain data, and generate predictions. The development of a model is guided by theory and observation to know which components are relevant to characterize the system's essential behavior. However, simplifying assumptions are needed to ensure the analysis is feasible and focused on the modeling objective. Simulations are employed to derive predictions from the model, often by varying the nature of the inputs to a model and observing its response.

**General Example:**
You are a student extremely curious about astrophysics. You love looking at the night sky and trying to find planets through a telescope, but you wonder where the optimal place to point your telescope would be such that you can see Jupiter in the sky. You decide to build a computer model of the solar system to help you make a prediction of where Jupiter will appear. You enumerate the bodies that will compose your system, namely the planets and the Sun, and you incorporate the astrophysical laws that guide their movement, like Kepler's Laws. Considering relevant variables and parameters of each of your agents, like their mass, distance from the sun, and speed, you create a robust system that accounts for the gravitational interactions between planets and the sun. You set your model to represent the current time of the year and consequently the current disposition of planets, which in turn helps you identify where Jupiter will be. You cross-reference that information with what you know about your current location on the Earth to infer where Jupiter will appear in the sky. Being computer-based, your model is capable of simulating how the passage of time will affect the orbits of planets in the solar system. Thus, you can now use it to predict the trajectory of any planet.

### #analogies

**Categorization:** Foundational Concept | Empirical Analyses | Thinking Creatively | Solving Problems

**Paragraph Description:**
Problems that appear superficially different can sometimes be recognized as structurally similar (e.g., a human heart versus a water pump or the atom versus the solar system). Although such reasoning has limitations, analogies can be invaluable in reasoning and problem solving. For example, once a problem has been solved, key elements of the solution can be abstracted and applied to solve other analogous problems using strategies such as reverse engineering. This process requires learning to ignore salient but idiosyncratic features that pertain only to a particular problem.

**General Example:**
Conservation ecologists have the problem of trying to determine which species are most important to the health and persistence of the overall ecological community. Despite obvious superficial differences between food webs and the internet, they realized there were some key similarities that might mean solutions to determining which webpages were the most important could be applied to determine which species are more important. Food webs are networks where the edges represent who eats whom in nature, whereas the internet is a network where edges represent webpages that link to one another. Because of this key structural similarity, the ecologists took the Google PageRank algorithm, which is an effective solution to determine the importance of webpages, and applied it to food webs. Importantly, they noted that one of the mathematical modifications Google made to account for the particular network structure of the internet could not realistically apply to food webs. They used the PageRank algorithm, but eliminated the mathematical modification and instead made a change to the network structure of food webs so that the PageRank algorithm could produce a solution of which species were most important to the health and persistence of the ecological community. Comparisons with other proposed solutions showed that the analogically derived PageRank solution outperformed others.

### #constraints

**Categorization:** Foundational Concept | Empirical Analyses | Thinking Creatively | Solving Problems

**Paragraph Description:**
A problem exists when one or more obstacles to a goal must be addressed. Obstacles often cannot be overcome easily, largely because there are constraints on what actions are possible or realistic. Constraints are requirements for the solution; they do not determine it but place boundaries on what is possible. Thus, part of solving a problem is identifying the constraints on what can be done. If the constraints are well-defined, problems can often be solved by devising ways to satisfy all of the constraints simultaneously. This approach to problem solving is known as "constraint satisfaction."

**General Example:**
You are moving into a new house and need to decide where to put the furniture in your bedroom. You have an old sofa that is missing a rear leg and sits on a telephone book, so you don't want to put it in a location where it can be seen from behind. You realize, however, there is no place to put the sofa so that it is not seen from behind, and so you decide to treat this as an obstacle rather than a constraint and get the sofa fixed. You budget so that you spend less money on groceries this week so you have enough money to overcome the obstacle: in other words, fix the sofa. You also have an old bed, with a headboard that must lean against the wall. Fixing the headboard would cost way more than fixing the sofa, so it is not feasible, and you decide to keep it as a constraint to be satisfied. Additionally, you have two end tables, which must be set to either side of the headboard. And a large chair and reading lamp, which must be near each other. Once you pick a wall for the headboard (one of the constraints), the positions of the other pieces of furniture may be determined. There is only one other place where the wall can accommodate the headboard, and once it and the end tables are set up, there is only one other place large enough for the chair and lamp. Because you fixed the sofa, you can put it anywhere where space is left. The mere act of satisfying all of the constraints and overcoming obstacles simultaneously almost dictates the solution to the problem.

### #heuristics

**Categorization:** Habit of Mind | Empirical Analyses | Thinking Creatively | Solving Problems

**Paragraph Description:**
Heuristics are simple, efficient rules that help us make decisions or solve problems and use limited information. They encompass conscious and unconscious strategies that help us take action under uncertainty, generate creative solutions, and quickly categorize or estimate entities. Heuristics do not guarantee correct or useful solutions but can often be efficient and effective tools when time and information are limited. As effective as they can be, heuristics commonly lead to systematic inaccuracies, known as biases. Identifying and understanding heuristics is crucial to fruitfully applying them in appropriate situations and avoiding them when they are likely to lead one astray.

**General Example:**
You work in a neuroscience lab, and your current assignment is to develop a protocol for immunohistochemical (IHC) analyses of human skin tissue. Despite being a common procedure in analyzing mouse skin tissues, a standard protocol has never been developed for the human tissue. You recognized that the existing protocols for mouse tissue may inform the overarching plan, so you decide to use the heuristic of analogical thinking to facilitate problem-solving. Keeping in mind the similarities and differences between human and mice anatomy, you noted three relevant steps that can be adopted from mice protocols to human protocols: tissue extraction, tissue processing, and antibody attachment. Then, you clearly defined the desirable end states for each of the three steps: samples of human tissue stored in 30% sucrose solution, 20-µm cross-sectional slices of human skin tissue, and ultimately the tissue slices with antibodies attached to the proteins of interest. From the third and final state, you work your way backwards to the second final state, so on and so forth until you reach the initial condition. You found multiple pathways from the final state to the initial state, and you tested each of them to identify the strengths and weaknesses of each before demonstrating the protocol to your supervisor.

### #scienceoflearning

**Categorization:** Habit of Mind | Empirical Analyses | Thinking Creatively | Solving Problems

**Paragraph Description:**
We not only learn in classes, but must continually learn new material to adapt to changing circumstances and expand our horizons. Some learning techniques (e.g., testing yourself) have proven very effective for many purposes, whereas others (e.g., underlining material in a book) have proven effective only for a narrow range of purposes. To know when to use a given technique, you must know why the techniques are effective for specific purposes—which relies on understanding principles from the science of learning. These principles are effective both for learning on your own and teaching others.

**General Example:**
You want to learn a foreign language. You use practice testing to learn vocabulary, knowing that it is successful in part because it elicits the generation effect; this effect states that information is more likely to be remembered if it is generated by one's own mind as opposed to being passively read from a source. You realize that you can improve your use of the practice testing learning technique by considering the learning principle of dual codes in addition to the learning principle of generation effect. Dual codes states that you are more likely to recall the same material if processed in two different media, because it enriches their retrieval cues. To incorporate dual codes into your use of practice testing, you create flashcards of pictures, to which you will answer the vocabulary word the picture represents. In addition, you space your practice out with increasing gaps, answering those vocabulary questions correctly multiple times to ensure you can easily use those words in conversation when you travel.

### #biasidentification

**Categorization:** Foundational Concept | Empirical Analyses | Thinking Critically | Analyzing Decisions

**Paragraph Description:**
Our understanding of the world depends on what we notice, focus on, and remember. Cognitive biases and emotional states can affect what we notice (or do not notice) and therefore influence our memories and decision making in various ways. For example, people have a tendency to filter out potentially useful information that does not correspond to their preconceived notions. In particular, we tend to seek out information that confirms or is consistent with our prior beliefs, disregard information that is contrary to them, and interpret equivocal information as supporting our beliefs. Heuristics—simple, efficient rules that can make problem solving and decision making faster and easier—can sometimes lead to biases and errors in judgment or reasoning and must be used with caution.

**General Example:**
You have a relative who is very nervous about a doctor's appointment, and asks you to go with him. Afterwards, he remembers what the doctor said as much more severe than you do. He was expecting to hear bad news, and thus kept thinking about everything that was even slightly negative, which distorted his memory. Furthermore, you noticed that your relative, instead of listening to the doctor, stared intently at a huge, gleaming syringe and needle that was placed over the counter and was thus subject to a top-down attention bias due to their emotional state. You realise that this attention bias made your relative filter out important, positive information and at the same time worsened their mood. You, on the other hand, took notes—and thus can correct his memory.

### #biasmitigation

**Categorization:** Habit of Mind | Empirical Analyses | Thinking Critically | Analyzing Decisions

**Paragraph Description:**
Biases can prevent us from considering important information when making decisions and drawing accurate conclusions. The key to minimizing the effects of bias is first to become aware of them and then determine how to reduce or eliminate their influence in a particular context. Learning to recognize common biases can help one to avoid them, but others may need to be mitigated by implementing specific processes. For this purpose, heuristics designed for preventing biases can be useful.

**General Example:**
You are working on the campaign team for a Presidential candidate whose policy platform includes sweeping reforms to address climate change. The team is focusing on strategies that can appeal to supporters of the opposition party, but they are concerned that confirmation bias from those voters will lead them to overlook and devalue your Candidate's arguments. While confirmation bias is certainly a core one to identify in this situation, you suggest the team also consider how emotional bias may interact with confirmation bias to make supporters of the opposition party more resistant than they otherwise would be. The political discourse has made voters on both sides of the aisle likely to feel anger when confronted with opposition views, and you point out that anger is an emotion associated with high certainty, which will only strengthen a person's existing confirmation bias. You explain that other emotions like surprise and fear are associated with low certainty, which would affect voters' depth of thought on the issue. By identifying voters' biases in a more nuanced way, the team now has clearer direction on how to shape a successful strategy. To mitigate those biases in the campaign materials about climate change, the team decides a few things. First, potential swing voters from the opposing party will be targeted with ads that only address the ideological issues without reference to the Candidate to reduce the opportunity for confirmation bias. Further, talking points from the opposing party in support of addressing climate change will be used in the ads to capitalize on confirmation bias. Even though humans have caused the current climate crisis, your materials avoid that language to reduce the emotional biases that might arise from anger about hot-button issues. Instead, the materials focus on how humans can be the solution to the problem, and interesting facts like how the US is a world leader in wind energy and the surprising number of jobs that industry provides. By avoiding language known to provoke anger among the target demographic, and including information that will provoke pride and surprise, you substantially reduce the emotional bias against your Candidate's climate change policies.

### #purpose

**Categorization:** Habit of Mind | Complex Systems | Thinking Critically | Analyzing Decisions

**Paragraph Description:**
Projects and initiatives frequently fail when they lack a clear and principled purpose. Identifying the purpose of an individual or group (including, but not limited to, formal organizations) is critical for evaluating their actions—or one's own. To construct an individual's or group's purpose, it is useful to articulate the underlying values that guide and constrain our goals. Moreover, successful outcomes also depend on articulating operating procedures, or guiding principles, consistent with the values and goals.

**General Example:**
The purpose of the non-profit organization that you want to volunteer in is "to create a radically inclusive, just and loving community, mobilized to alleviate suffering and break the cycles of poverty and marginalization." As you interrogate the organization and talk to its founders, you learn that one of their core values is the notion that all people deserve to be respected and empowered equally ('radically inclusive'), as well as a commitment to kind and pleasant interactions ('loving community'). Another core value is an egalitarian conception of social justice, according to which the way to respect and empower everyone equally is to ensure there are social conditions in place that prevent people from succumbing to poverty ('breaking the cycle of poverty and marginalization'). These values underlie their monthly goal of serving at least 60,000 meals to those who may otherwise go hungry. Likewise, their operating procedures reflect these values and goals as they give out free meals daily to anyone who comes to their walk-in center.

### #breakitdown

**Categorization:** Habit of Mind | Empirical Analyses | Thinking Creatively | Analyzing Problems

**Paragraph Description:**
Complex problems can seem overwhelming when taken as a whole, but become much easier to address when broken into components. However, not all ways of breaking a problem into subproblems are equally useful, nor do all invite equally effective strategies for solving the subproblems and assembling the solutions into a coherent whole.

**General Example:**
You are on a team tasked with drafting a governmental policy that will help alleviate the negative consequences of a local mining operation. However, you realize that the problem is too large to tackle. Thus, you break the main problem down by the type of problem (environmental degradation, residents' health concerns, and legal rights). An alternative way of breaking down the problem would be to use the "triple bottom line model" (financial sustainability, environmental sustainability, social sustainability). You realize that breaking down the problem by type makes it easier for different organizations to take action, so you proceeded with that breakdown. Your personal expertise and interests revolve around environmental impacts, so you decided to tackle the environmental degradation subproblem. Iterating the process, you break down the first order subproblem by type, forming second order subproblems (land pollution, water pollution, air pollution). Data from the United States Environmental Protection Agency suggests that the local air pollution is extreme enough to cause health issues. Hence, you decided that air pollution is the most urgent issue and proceeded with this subproblem. By focusing on the chemistry of pollutant molecules, the subproblem seemed tractable and you proceeded to the next stage of the policy-drafting process.

### #gapanalysis

**Categorization:** Foundational Concept | Empirical Analyses | Thinking Critically | Analyzing Problems

**Paragraph Description:**
A novel solution to a problem is only required when an adequate solution does not already exist. Thus, the first step in "filling a gap" with a new product, process, or procedure is determining whether there is a gap to be filled. This requires evaluating whether existing solutions are sufficient, can be suitably modified, or if a new solution is needed instead.

**General Example:**
A small agricultural NGO supports rural farmers and aims to increase crop yields. To address this goal, the NGO first considers using an existing, well-known mobile app that provides planting schedules, fertilizer recommendations, and pest alerts. However, they discover that the app was designed for large, commercial farms with reliable internet access and high-input farming systems. When the NGO tests it with their partner farmers, they find that many recommendations are unrealistic, depend on resources the farmers do not have, assume reliable connectivity, and sometimes encourage excessive fertilizer use. The NGO then evaluates several other existing digital farming tools, but encounters that despite using different approaches, all have similar issues. They all assume an infrastructure level that does not match the farmers' context. After critically assessing the current options and confirming that none can be used or adapted without losing usefulness, the NGO decides to design a simpler, offline-friendly tool that provides low-input recommendations tailored to local conditions. Once deployed, farmers report improved yields using guidance that finally matches what they are looking for.

### #rightproblem

**Categorization:** Habit of Mind | Empirical Analyses | Thinking Creatively | Analyzing Problems

**Paragraph Description:**
A crucial first step to solving a problem is to characterize exactly what the problem is. This helps to ensure that proposed solutions address the right problem—the one that needs to be solved. Characterizing a problem requires specifying the initial and goal states, the obstacles that stand between the starting situation and the goals, and the scale of the problem. Different types of solutions are appropriate for problems that arise at different scales.

**General Example:**
Events that ensure interaction between students and their rotation cities are at the heart of the Minerva experience. During the COVID-19 pandemic, these events were unable to happen as they originally did, as measures of social distancing and quarantine were in effect and many students studied remotely. Aiming to still promote student engagement and interactions, understanding the core problem was key to designing appropriate solutions. The initial state of the problem was that original in-city Minerva programs were canceled and students were at risk of feeling disconnected from their community. While some students were in rotation cities, a large percentage were totally remote. The goal state was for all students to have opportunities to engage with their class, feel a sense of belonging, and connect their academic experience to their student life experience. The scale of the problem concerned the whole Minerva community, internationally, though considering the problem at the cohort scale also seemed useful. The most relevant obstacles included (1) fearful mindsets about the pandemic, (2) the pandemic added uncertainty to planning, and (3) student routines and needs changed.

### #plausibility

**Categorization:** Habit of Mind | Empirical Analyses | Thinking Creatively | Applying Research Methods

**Paragraph Description:**
Evaluating whether a hypothesis is plausible requires the ability to identify well-constructed hypotheses. Such hypotheses rest on well-defended and internally consistent premises or assumptions and are consistent with well-established scientific results. One should learn to identify hypotheses that lack such solid foundations and recognize that they are implausible.

**General Example:**
A norovirus has infected many students in the residence hall. Symptoms include vomiting, diarrhea, and low-grade fever. You begin to feel nauseous and feverish. A friend says that he had the norovirus for a few days, but started taking an antibiotic that he had leftover from a previous illness and he hypothesizes that the antibiotic helped him overcome the norovirus. He tells you to go get some. In your feverish state you think about this hypothesis, but decide to disregard his advice because it is based on an implausible assumption that antibiotics can kill viruses. You consider that in some cases viruses act in symbiosis with bacteria, and further that a virus-weakened immune system could in theory be subject to opportunistic bacterial infections that could be causing your illness. Nonetheless, you recognize that these possibilities lack in parsimony, and that you lack the hallmarks of bacterial infection like production of pus and inflammation. Finally, you conclude that it is more plausible that your friend recovered because norovirus illness typically resolves in a few days.

### #testability

**Categorization:** Habit | Empirical Analysis | Thinking Critically | Evaluating Claims

**Paragraph Description:**
Evaluating whether a hypothesis is testable requires the ability to identify hypotheses that generate testable predictions. One must learn to identify hypotheses that do not lead to testable predictions and hence cannot be evaluated. One should also be able to generate multiple predictions from a hypothesis and determine how well they will allow us to conclude whether a hypothesis is supported or refuted.

**General Example:**
You work on a local politician's campaign team for the upcoming local elections. You suggest employing more door-to-door campaigning will increase support among residents because they seek more face-to-face interactions. Your colleague argues that while your proposal makes sense, you don't have the means to determine the impact of door-to-door visits. Your colleague is suggesting that your hypothesis that increasing face-to-face interactions will cause an increase in support among residents is untestable. First, you suggest randomizing the intervention (door-to-door campaigning) among the city's neighborhoods. Then, you inform the other campaign channels to keep the same strategy over the research period. You predict that there will be a larger increase in support among residents in the neighborhoods that received the door-to-door intervention compared to the other campaign channels (control groups). While your hypothesis is testable in principle, however, you still need to find out whether it is testable in practice. You ask your manager how much budget you would be able to allot for this experiment and they give you a sufficient amount to cover all expenses. You then investigate how long your experiment would take and realize you would need to have the results done by the end of the campaign. Since the entire research project would take around 2 months, and the elections are in 4 months time, your hypothesis is determined to be testable in both principle and practice. To improve the testability of the hypothesis even more, you generate a second prediction: that polling numbers will increase significantly in the neighborhoods with door-to-door campaigning compared to the neighborhoods without. Both of these predictions are useful while testing your hypothesis.
