# #connotation

## Rubric definition

Understand and use connotations, tone, and style.

## Strong example — body application

You are writing a campaign advertisement about police brutality in your hometown in response to recent murders of innocent citizens, and are trying to decide between the words "strong" and "forceful" to describe the police force. The denotative meanings of such words are close enough that in principle they often might be substituted for each other — but their connotations are very different, which cautions against simply using a thesaurus to select words. Because you are trying to raise awareness of unwarranted use of force by police officers, you wish to portray them as aggressive. You therefore choose to go with the word "forceful" because the connotative meaning is slightly more negative, successfully antagonizing the police force for your purpose, as opposed to using "strong", which might have a positive connotation and portray them as protectors instead of abusers.

## Strong example — contextualization footnote

The diction chosen to describe the police force was carefully selected to connote a body of unnecessarily aggressive officers (e.g. "forceful"), thus portraying them in a negative light and contributing to the purpose of your campaign as opposed to other word choices that could unintentionally glorify them (e.g. "strong").

## Why this pair works

- **Two options are explicitly compared.** "Forceful" vs. "strong" — the footnote doesn't just name the chosen word, it names the rejected alternative and explains the connotative difference between them.
- **Purpose is tied to word choice.** The selection isn't arbitrary; it directly serves the campaign goal of portraying the police negatively. The "because" link is explicit.
- **Connotative, not just denotative, reasoning.** The footnote acknowledges both words have similar denotations but argues connotation is what drives the choice — demonstrating awareness of the HC's core distinction.
- **Effect on reader is specified.** "Portray them as protectors instead of abusers" — the footnote describes what would go wrong with the alternative, not just what goes right with the choice.

## Evaluation criteria

### Strong body application
- Contains a deliberate word, phrase, or tonal choice where connotation visibly matters
- The choice aligns with the purpose and intended effect on the reader/audience
- An alternative phrasing with different connotations would noticeably change the piece's tone or impact

### Strong footnote contextualization
- Names the specific word or phrase chosen
- Names at least one alternative that was (or could have been) rejected
- Explains the connotative difference between them
- Links that difference to the purpose of the piece ("because I want the reader to feel X")
- Goes beyond "I chose words carefully" — must show the comparison and the reasoning

## Common weaknesses — flag these when you see them

| Weakness | What it looks like | Stronger version |
|---|---|---|
| No alternative offered | "I chose 'forceful' for its negative connotation." | "I chose 'forceful' over 'strong' because 'strong' connotes protection, which would undermine the campaign's goal." |
| Denotation confused with connotation | "I used precise vocabulary." | Connotation is about emotional/cultural association, not accuracy. The footnote must address feeling/tone, not just meaning. |
| Purpose link missing | "Forceful has a more negative connotation." | So what? Explain why negative connotation serves *this* piece's goal. |
| Generic tone claim | "I matched the tone to my audience." | Name the tone (aggressive, ironic, deferential) and the specific word/phrase that creates it. |
