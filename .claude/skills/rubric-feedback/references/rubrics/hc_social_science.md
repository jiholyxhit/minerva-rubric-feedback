### #levelsofanalysis

**Categorization:** Habit of Mind | Complex Systems | Interacting Effectively | Interacting with Complex Systems

**Paragraph Description:**
Research conducted on different scales of analysis often reveals patterns that are not visible, or are insufficient to explain, at one level. The micro level focuses on small-scale components of a system, while the macro level examines large-scale patterns. Meso levels are between micro and macro. Findings from one level of analysis do not necessarily explain findings at another level, and this disparity must be accounted for when analyzing complex systems.

**General Example:**
An Adobe employee satisfaction nonprofit is planning outreach campaigns. The team analyzes satisfaction at the individual (micro) level by looking at survey results for each employee. They also analyze at the department (meso) level by grouping data by team. Finally, they look at company-wide (macro) trends. They notice that the macro data suggests overall high satisfaction, but the micro data reveals certain employee groups are highly dissatisfied—a finding that would be missed by only looking at the macro level.

### #shapingbehavior

**Categorization:** Habit of Mind | Complex Systems | Interacting Effectively | Negotiating and Persuading

**Paragraph Description:**
Incentives, disincentives, rewards, punishments, and choice architecture can all be used to shape behavior. Incentives encourage behavior by providing positive reinforcement, while disincentives discourage behavior through negative reinforcement or punishment. Choice architecture refers to the way choices are presented to people, which can influence their decisions without restricting options.

**General Example:**
A city government wants to reduce cigarette smoking among residents. Rather than banning smoking outright, they implement a combination of strategies: they raise taxes on cigarettes (disincentive), offer free nicotine patches at pharmacies (incentive), and redesign public spaces so that smoking areas are inconveniently located far from main pedestrian areas (choice architecture). These layered approaches shape behavior without outright prohibition.

### #systemmapping

**Categorization:** Foundational Concept | Complex Systems | Interacting Effectively | Interacting with complex systems

**Paragraph Description:**
According to Plato, there is no obvious way to "carve nature at its joints"—any complex system can be decomposed in multiple ways. System mapping involves identifying the components of a system, the relationships between those components, and the boundaries of the system. A good system map makes explicit the functional units and their interactions, revealing leverage points and potential inefficiencies.

**General Example:**
A hospital administrator creates a system map of the hospital's spending. By diagramming all departments, their costs, and their interdependencies, the administrator identifies that two departments are running nearly identical educational campaigns for patients—a redundancy that could be eliminated to save costs. The system map made this overlap visible in a way that reviewing individual budgets could not.

### #emergentproperties

**Categorization:** Foundational Concept | Complex Systems | Interacting Effectively | Understanding and predicting complex patterns.

**Paragraph Description:**
Some properties of complex systems can be easily understood as trivial combinations of parts, but emergent properties are those that arise from the interactions of components and cannot be predicted from or reduced to the properties of individual parts alone. Emergence is a key feature of complex systems, from ant colonies to economies to consciousness.

**General Example:**
At a Minerva hackathon (M3P), students who had learned Python and students who had learned JavaScript were placed in mixed groups. Individually, neither group could build a full-stack application. But through their interactions and complementary skills, the groups produced applications that neither could have created alone—an emergent outcome from the combination of different knowledge bases.

### #complexcausality

**Categorization:** Foundational Concept | Complex Systems | Interacting Effectively | Interacting with Complex Systems

**Paragraph Description:**
Complex causality involves multiple causes and interactions producing secondary, tertiary, and higher-order effects. In complex systems, effects can feed back to become causes (feedback loops), causes can interact non-linearly, and outcomes can be disproportionate to their initial triggers. Understanding complex causality is essential for diagnosing problems and designing effective interventions.

**General Example:**
Analysts studying San Francisco homelessness identify multiple interacting causes: rising rents, mental health crises, inadequate shelter capacity, drug addiction, and insufficient social services. These causes interact—mental illness can lead to job loss, which leads to inability to pay rent, which leads to homelessness, which worsens mental illness. Any single-cause explanation or single-intervention solution misses this complexity.

### #networks

**Categorization:** Foundational Concept | Complex Systems | Interacting Effectively | Understanding and predicting complex patterns

**Paragraph Description:**
Network analysis examines relationships and connections among agents (nodes) through edges (connections). Networks can reveal patterns of influence, information flow, and vulnerability. Key metrics include degree centrality (number of connections), betweenness centrality (how often a node lies on shortest paths), and clustering coefficients. Understanding network structure helps predict behavior and design interventions.

**General Example:**
An intern wants to spread a new idea through their office but doesn't know whom to approach first. By mapping informal communication networks—who talks to whom at lunch, whose advice people seek—they identify a "hub" person who connects many otherwise disconnected colleagues. By convincing this hub person first, the intern efficiently spreads the idea throughout the organization.

### #systemdynamics

**Categorization:** Foundational Concept | Complex Systems | Interacting Effectively | Interacting within Complex Systems

**Paragraph Description:**
Phase spaces describe and predict changes in complex systems over time. System dynamics involves stocks (accumulations), flows (rates of change), and feedback loops (reinforcing or balancing). By modeling these elements, one can understand how systems evolve, identify tipping points, and anticipate unintended consequences of interventions.

**General Example:**
The mayor of Lakeville, a town that had been economically floundering, used system dynamics thinking to understand why previous economic interventions had failed. By mapping reinforcing loops (e.g., unemployment leads to decreased spending, which leads to more business closures and more unemployment) and identifying leverage points, the mayor designed an intervention that broke the negative reinforcing loop, eventually leading the town to flourish economically.

### #negotiate

**Categorization:** Habit of Mind | Complex Systems | Interacting Effectively | Negotiating and Persuading

**Paragraph Description:**
Effective negotiation involves clearly identifying interests (underlying needs and desires) and areas of common ground, as distinct from positions (stated demands). Interest-based negotiation (as opposed to positional bargaining) opens up creative solutions that satisfy both parties. Key strategies include active listening, separating people from problems, and generating options for mutual gain.

**General Example:**
A factory wants to build a new facility. The city government objects on environmental grounds. Rather than treating it as a zero-sum dispute, the negotiating teams identify shared interests: the city wants clean air and economic growth; the factory wants to operate efficiently and maintain community goodwill. By focusing on these shared interests, they reach an agreement that includes the factory installing advanced emissions controls in exchange for streamlined permitting.

### #ethicalconsiderations

**Categorization:** Habit of Mind | Complex Systems | Interacting Effectively | Resolving Ethical Problems

**Paragraph Description:**
Many decisions involve ethical considerations—questions about what is right, fair, or good. Ethical frameworks such as consequentialism (focus on outcomes), deontology (focus on duties and rules), and virtue ethics (focus on character) provide different lenses for evaluating decisions. Identifying affected stakeholders, weighing harms and benefits, and considering rights and duties are all part of ethical analysis.

**General Example:**
A game design studio is developing a mobile game called WhoSolvedItFirst and considering implementing microtransactions that would give paying players significant advantages. The team identifies ethical considerations: the mechanic may exploit children who cannot understand the financial implications, create unfair advantages based on wealth rather than skill, and undermine the game's educational value. Applying consequentialist reasoning, they redesign the monetization to avoid pay-to-win mechanics.

### #ethicalcourage

**Categorization:** Habit of Mind | Complex Systems | Interacting Effectively | Resolving Ethical Problems

**Paragraph Description:**
It is often challenging to figure out the right thing to do in complex situations, and even harder to act on that judgment when facing social pressure, authority, or personal cost. Ethical courage involves recognizing when ethical action is required, determining the right course of action, and having the fortitude to act on that judgment despite potential costs or resistance.

**General Example:**
A quiet student in a residence hall is being targeted by anonymous bullying through an app. Most students are aware of the situation but say nothing because they fear social backlash. One student decides it is ethically necessary to speak up, reports the behavior to residential advisors, and publicly advocates for a community norm against anonymous harassment—despite the social risk. This action shifts the community's norms and stops the bullying.

### #ethicaljudgment

**Categorization:** (none listed)

**Paragraph Description:**
Ethical questions can be difficult to answer because they involve competing values, uncertain consequences, and complex stakeholder interests. Good ethical judgment requires carefully identifying the ethical dimensions of a situation, applying relevant ethical frameworks, considering the perspectives of all affected parties, and making a reasoned decision even under uncertainty.

**General Example:**
A tech company is asked by law enforcement to unlock the phone of a mass shooting suspect. The company faces competing ethical considerations: cooperating could help prevent future violence (consequentialist argument for compliance), but creating a backdoor could compromise the privacy and security of all users (rights-based argument against compliance). The company must exercise careful ethical judgment in weighing these competing values.

### #conformity

**Categorization:** Habit | Complex Systems | Interacting Effectively | Working With Others

**Paragraph Description:**
Humans have a "herd instinct"—a tendency to conform to group norms and behaviors. Conformity can be informational (following others because they seem to have better information) or normative (following others to gain social acceptance). Understanding conformity is crucial for predicting social behavior and designing effective interventions that leverage or counteract it.

**General Example:**
Students in a residence hall want to improve kitchen cleanliness. Rather than posting rules or assigning blame, they launch a norm-change campaign. They post signs showing that "most students in this hall clean up after themselves within 15 minutes" (using descriptive norms) and leave thank-you notes when the kitchen is clean. By making the prosocial norm visible and positive, they leverage conformity to improve behavior.

### #differences

**Categorization:** Habit of Mind | Complex Systems | Interacting Effectively | Working With Others

**Paragraph Description:**
People differ in skills, attitudes, beliefs, identity, and social position in ways that affect how they work together. These differences can be sources of both conflict and creative strength. Effective collaboration involves recognizing and valuing relevant differences, adapting communication styles, and designing processes that allow diverse team members to contribute their distinctive strengths.

**General Example:**
A transportation hub design team includes Amar, who is detail-oriented and focused on safety specifications, and Bo, who is a natural communicator focused on user experience. Initially their different styles cause friction. A project manager helps them see how their differences are complementary: Amar's attention to detail catches safety issues while Bo's communication skills keep stakeholders aligned. By structuring collaboration to leverage both, the team produces a superior design.

### #emotionaliq

**Categorization:** Habit | Complex Systems | Interacting Effectively | Working with others

**Paragraph Description:**
Emotional intelligence (EQ) is the ability to be aware of, evaluate, and manage one's own emotions and the emotions of others. High EQ involves perceiving emotions accurately, using emotions to facilitate thought, understanding emotional meaning and causes, and managing emotions effectively. EQ is a stronger predictor of professional success in collaborative environments than IQ alone.

**General Example:**
A project manager notices that afternoon team meetings are consistently unproductive and contentious. Recognizing that mid-afternoon hunger ("hanger") may be affecting team members' emotional states and decision-making quality, they start providing popcorn at afternoon meetings. This small intervention improves emotional regulation, reduces conflict, and significantly improves meeting outcomes.

### #leadprinciples

**Categorization:** Habit | Complex Systems | Interacting Effectively | Working With Others

**Paragraph Description:**
Leadership always entails the exercise of power—the capacity to influence others. Effective leadership involves articulating a compelling vision, building trust through consistency and integrity, empowering others rather than centralizing control, adapting leadership style to context, and managing the ethical dimensions of power. Understanding leadership principles requires studying both effective and ineffective leaders.

**General Example:**
A political science student analyzes a historical fascist leader to understand leadership principles. They examine how the leader built a mass movement by articulating a powerful (if destructive) vision, exploiting fear and economic insecurity, using charisma and propaganda to build personal loyalty, centralizing power and eliminating checks and balances, and systematically dismantling institutional constraints. This analysis illuminates how leadership principles can be used for harmful ends.

### #powerdynamics

**Categorization:** Habit | Complex Systems | Interacting Effectively | Working With Others

**Paragraph Description:**
Power is one's ability to get others to do what they otherwise wouldn't. Power can be formal (authority from position) or informal (influence from expertise, relationships, or information). Power dynamics shape interactions within organizations and social systems. Understanding power involves recognizing its sources, how it flows, how it can be used, and how it can be resisted or redistributed.

**General Example:**
At a participatory budgeting meeting, community members are nominally empowered to allocate city funds. However, a facilitator observes that certain well-organized groups with prior experience dominate the discussion, while newer community members and non-English speakers are effectively sidelined. By recognizing these informal power dynamics, the facilitator restructures the process—using small group discussions, translation services, and anonymous voting—to more equitably distribute decision-making power.

### #responsibility

**Categorization:** Habit of Mind | Complex Systems | Interacting Effectively | Working With Others

**Paragraph Description:**
Taking responsibility for one's own contributions and communicating proactively are essential to effective collaboration. Responsibility involves following through on commitments, acknowledging mistakes rather than deflecting blame, seeking feedback, and managing others' expectations through clear and timely communication. Responsibility also includes recognizing the limits of one's own capacity and asking for help when needed.

**General Example:**
A student team working on a final project has one member who consistently misses deadlines and attends meetings unprepared. Rather than working around this person, the team leader has a direct conversation, explicitly states how the behavior is affecting the project, asks about barriers the person may be facing, and co-creates a revised plan with clear accountability mechanisms. This direct responsibility-focused intervention resolves the problem.

### #selfawareness

**Categorization:** Habit of Mind | Complex Systems | Interacting Effectively | Understanding and predicting complex patterns

**Paragraph Description:**
Humans generally form overly optimistic assessments of their own characteristics, knowledge, and abilities—a phenomenon known as illusory superiority. Self-awareness involves accurately assessing one's own skills, biases, emotional states, and impact on others. Seeking external feedback, keeping records of one's performance, and actively looking for disconfirming evidence are strategies for improving self-awareness.

**General Example:**
During a group assignment, a student consistently avoids the coding tasks, claiming they aren't needed. When peers provide feedback at the end of the project, multiple teammates note that this avoidance put unfair burden on others. The feedback is uncomfortable but prompts the student to recognize a pattern of avoiding challenging technical work and make a concrete plan to develop coding skills in future projects.

### #strategize

**Categorization:** Habit | Complex Systems | Interacting Effectively | Working With Others

**Paragraph Description:**
Choosing how to act requires strategizing—analyzing one's situation, identifying goals and constraints, assessing the competitive landscape, and selecting actions designed to achieve desired outcomes given what others are likely to do. Good strategy involves diagnosis (understanding the situation), a guiding policy (overall approach), and coherent actions (specific steps that reinforce each other).

**General Example:**
A political candidate in a crowded primary decides to strategically position themselves as an ideologically pure outsider, rather than competing for the mainstream moderate vote where several strong candidates already compete. By analyzing the competitive landscape, identifying an underserved voter segment, and consistently communicating a distinctive message, the candidate builds a passionate base that punches above its numeric weight.

### #psychologicalexplanation

**Categorization:** Foundational Concept | Complex Systems | Thinking Critically | Analyzing Decision

**Paragraph Description:**
Within social systems, each human is a complex agent whose behavior can be explained by psychological theories. Psychological explanations draw on theories of cognition, emotion, motivation, and social influence to account for why people behave as they do. Major frameworks include Beck's Cognitive Model (which links situations, thoughts, emotions, and behaviors), self-determination theory, and social learning theory.

**General Example:**
A nonprofit employee is confused why a new colleague seems hostile. The employee later learns the colleague saw them eating the colleague's labeled yogurt on the first day—an accident the employee didn't notice. Using Beck's Cognitive Model, the employee understands: the situation (missing yogurt) triggered the thought "this person disrespects me," which triggered the emotion of anger, which produced avoidant hostile behavior. The employee buys the colleague a 6-pack of yogurt and apologizes, resolving the misunderstanding.

### #purpose

**Categorization:** Habit of Mind | Complex Systems | Thinking Critically | Analyzing Decisions

**Paragraph Description:**
Projects and initiatives frequently fail when they lack a clear and principled purpose. Identifying the purpose of an individual or group (including, but not limited to, formal organizations) is critical for evaluating their actions—or one's own. To construct an individual's or group's purpose, it is useful to articulate the underlying values that guide and constrain our goals. Moreover, successful outcomes also depend on articulating operating procedures, or guiding principles, consistent with the values and goals.

**General Example:**
The purpose of the non-profit organization that you want to volunteer in is "to create a radically inclusive, just and loving community, mobilized to alleviate suffering and break the cycles of poverty and marginalization." As you interrogate the organization and talk to its founders, you learn that one of their core values is the notion that all people deserve to be respected and empowered equally ('radically inclusive'), as well as a commitment to kind and pleasant interactions ('loving community'). Another core value is an egalitarian conception of social justice, according to which the way to respect and empower everyone equally is to ensure there are social conditions in place that prevent people from succumbing to poverty ('breaking the cycle of poverty and marginalization'). These values underlie their monthly goal of serving at least 60,000 meals to those who may otherwise go hungry. Likewise, their operating procedures reflect these values and goals as they give out free meals daily to anyone who comes to their walk-in center.
